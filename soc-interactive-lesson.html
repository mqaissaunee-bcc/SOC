Module 01

What Is a Security Operations Center?

The SOC is the nerve center of an organization's cybersecurity defense. It's where threats are detected, analyzed, and neutralized in real time — 24 hours a day, 365 days a year.

🏢 The Mission

A Security Operations Center (SOC) is a centralized facility — or virtual team — responsible for continuously monitoring, detecting, analyzing, and responding to cybersecurity threats across an organization's entire IT infrastructure. Think of it as a command center for digital defense.

The SOC exists to reduce the time between a breach happening and the organization knowing about it (called dwell time). The faster threats are discovered, the less damage they cause.

⚙️ Core Functions

Every SOC performs five essential functions that form a continuous security cycle:

Step 1Monitor
→
Step 2Detect
→
Step 3Analyze
→
Step 4Respond
→
Step 5Improve

Monitor: Continuously watch networks, endpoints, servers, and cloud environments for suspicious activity.

Detect: Use automated tools and human analysis to identify potential threats from the flood of data.

Analyze: Investigate detected events to determine if they are real attacks or harmless false positives.

Respond: Contain threats, eradicate malicious activity, and recover affected systems.

Improve: Document lessons learned, update detection rules, and strengthen defenses for the future.

Key Concept A SOC's effectiveness is measured by metrics like Mean Time to Detect (MTTD) and Mean Time to Respond (MTTR). Faster detection and response means less damage from cyberattacks.

🌐 Why SOCs Matter

Modern organizations face thousands of security events every day. Without a SOC, attacks can go undetected for weeks or even months. The average SOC receives tens of thousands of alerts daily — but only a small fraction point to real threats. The SOC team's job is to find those real needles in an enormous haystack of data.

SOCs protect sensitive data (customer records, financial information, intellectual property), ensure regulatory compliance (HIPAA, PCI-DSS, GDPR), maintain business continuity, and serve as the organization's first line of defense against cyber criminals, nation-state actors, and insider threats.

Module 02

The SOC Team Structure

SOC teams are organized into tiers based on experience and responsibility. Each tier handles different types of work, from initial alert review to advanced threat hunting.

Tier 1 — Triage Analyst

Alert Monitoring & Initial Response

The front line. Tier 1 analysts are the first to see alerts as they come in and make the initial call on whether they're real threats.

  • Monitor SIEM dashboards 24/7
  • Review and classify incoming alerts
  • Distinguish true positives from false positives
  • Escalate confirmed threats to Tier 2
  • Document all triage decisions
Tier 2 — Incident Responder

Deep Investigation & Containment

When Tier 1 escalates a confirmed threat, Tier 2 dives deeper to understand the full scope and contain the damage.

  • Perform deep-dive investigation of escalated alerts
  • Determine scope and impact of incidents
  • Use threat intelligence (IOCs, TTPs) for correlation
  • Coordinate containment and remediation
  • Escalate critical incidents to Tier 3
Tier 3 — Threat Hunter

Proactive Hunting & Advanced Analysis

The most experienced analysts. They don't wait for alerts — they proactively search for threats hiding in the network.

  • Proactively hunt for unknown threats
  • Perform vulnerability assessments
  • Conduct malware reverse engineering
  • Optimize detection rules and tools
  • Lead response for critical breaches
Your Role in the Simulation During the SOC simulation exercises, you'll primarily work at the Tier 1 level (alert triage and monitoring), progressing through Tier 2 (incident investigation) and into Tier 3 (threat hunting) activities as you advance through the training scenarios.

👥 Additional SOC Roles

SOC Manager: Supervises the entire team, sets priorities, creates processes, handles staffing, and reports security posture to executive leadership (the CISO).

Security Engineer: Builds and maintains the tools and infrastructure the analysts use — SIEM rules, firewall configurations, detection signatures, and automation playbooks.

Forensics Analyst: Investigates completed incidents to reconstruct what happened, preserve evidence, and support legal or compliance proceedings.

Malware Analyst: Reverse-engineers malicious software to understand how it works, what it does, and how to detect it in the future.

Module 03

Core SOC Tools & Technologies

SOC analysts rely on a suite of specialized tools to monitor, detect, and respond to threats. Here are the essential technologies you'll encounter in the simulation.

🔍 SIEM — Security Information & Event Management

▼

The SIEM is the central nervous system of the SOC. It collects, aggregates, and correlates log data from every device on the network — servers, firewalls, endpoints, applications, cloud services — and analyzes it to detect suspicious patterns.

What it does: Ingests millions of log events per day. Applies correlation rules to identify attack patterns. Generates alerts when suspicious activity is detected. Provides dashboards for real-time visibility. Stores historical data for forensic investigation.

In your simulation: Screen 1 (SIEM Dashboard) shows the alert queue, severity filters, alert volume trends, and MITRE ATT&CK heatmap. This is where you'll spend most of your time as a Tier 1 analyst.

Real-world examples: Splunk, IBM QRadar, Microsoft Sentinel, Elastic SIEM, LogRhythm.

🌐 Network Monitoring & IDS/IPS

▼

Network monitoring tools watch all traffic flowing through the organization's network. They look for suspicious patterns, unauthorized connections, and known malicious traffic signatures.

IDS (Intrusion Detection System) passively monitors traffic and generates alerts when it sees something suspicious. IPS (Intrusion Prevention System) goes further by actively blocking malicious traffic in real time.

What to watch for: Unusual traffic volumes or destinations. Connections to known malicious IP addresses. Port scanning activity. Data exfiltration patterns (large outbound transfers). Command-and-control (C2) beaconing behavior.

In your simulation: Screen 2 (Network Monitor) shows the global threat map, live traffic graphs, firewall logs, top talkers, and port activity.

Real-world examples: Snort, Suricata, Zeek (Bro), Palo Alto NGFW, Wireshark for packet analysis.

🚨 Incident Response Platform

▼

When an alert is confirmed as a real incident, it moves into the incident response platform. This is where the SOC team manages the full lifecycle of a security incident — from detection through containment, eradication, recovery, and lessons learned.

Key capabilities: Case management and ticketing for tracking incidents. Timeline reconstruction to understand the attack sequence. Playbooks with step-by-step response procedures. Communication and escalation workflows. Evidence collection and chain-of-custody tracking.

In your simulation: Screen 3 (Incident Response Console) shows active cases, IOC details, affected assets, response actions, and response playbooks.

Real-world examples: ServiceNow SecOps, Palo Alto XSOAR, Splunk SOAR, TheHive, IBM Resilient.

🧠 Threat Intelligence Platform

▼

Threat intelligence is information about known threats — who the attackers are, what tools they use, what they target, and how they operate. A threat intelligence platform collects, organizes, and distributes this information to the SOC team.

Types of threat intelligence: Strategic — high-level trends for leadership. Tactical — TTPs (tactics, techniques, procedures) for security teams. Operational — details about specific campaigns or attacks. Technical — specific IOCs like malicious IP addresses, file hashes, and domains.

In your simulation: Screen 4 (Threat Intel & Endpoints) shows live threat feeds, endpoint health status, vulnerability management data, and compliance scores.

Real-world examples: Recorded Future, Mandiant Threat Intelligence, MISP (open-source), AlienVault OTX, VirusTotal.

💻 EDR — Endpoint Detection & Response

▼

EDR solutions are installed on individual endpoints (laptops, desktops, servers) to monitor and protect them against threats at the device level. They provide visibility into what's happening on each machine in real time.

Key capabilities: Real-time monitoring of processes, file changes, and network connections on endpoints. Behavioral analysis to detect suspicious activity that signature-based tools miss. Remote isolation of compromised machines. Forensic data collection from endpoints. Automated response actions like killing processes or quarantining files.

In your simulation: Endpoint status data appears on Screen 4, showing which machines are healthy, which have active agents, and which may be compromised.

Real-world examples: CrowdStrike Falcon, SentinelOne, Microsoft Defender for Endpoint, Carbon Black.

Module 04

The MITRE ATT&CK Framework

ATT&CK is the industry-standard language for describing how cyberattacks work. Understanding it is essential for every SOC analyst.

📖 What is ATT&CK?

MITRE ATT&CK (Adversarial Tactics, Techniques, and Common Knowledge) is a globally accessible knowledge base that catalogs real-world adversary behaviors. It was created by the MITRE Corporation based on observations of actual cyberattacks.

Think of it as a dictionary of attack methods. It gives every type of attacker action a standard name and ID number, so security teams worldwide can communicate clearly about threats. When your SIEM dashboard shows an alert mapped to "T1059 — Command and Scripting Interpreter," every analyst knows exactly what type of behavior was detected.

🧩 The Three Building Blocks

Tactics — The "why." These are the attacker's high-level goals at each phase of an attack. Think of them as the columns of the ATT&CK matrix. Example: "Initial Access" is the tactic of getting a foothold in the target network.

Techniques — The "how." These are the specific methods attackers use to achieve each tactic. Example: Under "Initial Access," one technique is "Phishing" (T1566) — sending deceptive emails to trick users into opening malicious attachments.

Procedures — The "exact steps." These are specific implementations of a technique by a particular threat group. Example: APT29 (a Russian state-sponsored group) uses spear-phishing emails with malicious links as their specific phishing procedure.

Module 05

Indicators of Compromise (IOCs)

IOCs are the digital breadcrumbs that attackers leave behind. Learning to recognize them is one of the most important skills for any SOC analyst.

🔎 What are IOCs?

An Indicator of Compromise (IOC) is a piece of forensic evidence that suggests a system or network may have been breached. IOCs are the clues that help analysts detect, investigate, and respond to cyberattacks.

IOCs are reactive by nature — they're evidence that something has already happened. This is different from Indicators of Attack (IOA), which signal that an attack may be happening right now. A good SOC uses both together.

📂 Types of IOCs

Network-Based

Suspicious network activity and traffic patterns

Malicious IP: 185.220.101.xx
C2 domain: evil-update.xyz
Host-Based

Suspicious activity on individual computers/servers

Registry key: HKLM\...\Run
Unexpected process: svchost2.exe
File-Based

Malicious files, suspicious hashes, or altered system files

MD5: a3f2b8c...d4e1f0
File: invoice.pdf.exe
Behavioral

Unusual patterns in user or system behavior

500 failed logins in 2 min
Admin login at 3:00 AM
Email-Based

Signs of phishing or email compromise

Spoofed sender domain
Macro-enabled .docm attachment
Geographic

Activity from unexpected locations

Login from 2 countries in 1 hr
Traffic to sanctioned nations

Module 06

Alert Triage & Prioritization

With thousands of alerts per day, knowing which to investigate first is the most critical skill for a Tier 1 analyst. Let's practice.

📊 Severity Levels

Alerts are classified by severity — a quick indicator of how dangerous the threat is and how fast you need to act.

CriticalImmediate action required. Active breach, ransomware execution, confirmed data exfiltration. Drop everything and respond.
HighUrgent investigation. Strong indicators of compromise, credential theft, malware detected. Investigate within minutes.
MediumInvestigate soon. Suspicious activity that could be malicious — unusual DNS, policy violations, anomalous behavior.
LowMonitor and document. Minor policy violations, single failed logins, low-risk anomalies. Review during normal workflow.
InfoAwareness only. Routine events, completed scans, system health updates. No action typically needed.

🧭 The Triage Decision Process

1New Alert Arrives
→
2Check Severity
→
3Validate: Real or False Positive?
→
4Investigate Context
→
5Act or Escalate

Step 1 — New alert arrives: The SIEM generates an alert based on a detection rule matching suspicious activity.

Step 2 — Check severity: Look at the assigned severity level. Critical and High alerts get immediate attention.

Step 3 — Validate: Is this a true positive (real threat) or false positive (harmless activity triggering a rule)? Check if the source is a known security scanner, scheduled task, or legitimate business process.

Step 4 — Investigate context: Cross-reference with other data sources. Check the threat intel feed. Look at the user's history. Review network traffic for corroborating evidence.

Step 5 — Act or escalate: If confirmed as benign, mark as false positive and document. If confirmed as a real threat, begin containment or escalate to Tier 2.

Analyst Tip — Identifying False Positives Common sources of false positives: authorized security scanners and vulnerability assessment tools, scheduled backup jobs running at night, legitimate software updates downloading large files, IT admin accounts performing authorized maintenance, marketing tools syncing data with cloud services. Always check the asset inventory and the user's role before classifying an alert.

Knowledge Check

Test your understanding before heading into the simulation. Answer these questions to verify you're ready.

📝 Final Assessment

Score: 0 / 10

Q1. What does SIEM stand for?

SIEM stands for Security Information and Event Management. It collects and correlates log data from across the network to detect threats and generate alerts for analysts.

Q2. Which SOC tier is responsible for initial alert triage and monitoring?

Tier 1 analysts are the front line — they monitor SIEM dashboards, review incoming alerts, classify them as true or false positives, and escalate confirmed threats to Tier 2.

Q3. In the MITRE ATT&CK framework, what do "Tactics" represent?

Tactics are the "why" — the attacker's high-level goals at each phase. Techniques are the "how," and Procedures are the specific implementation details.

Q4. A scheduled vulnerability scan from an authorized IT security tool triggers a "port scan detected" alert. This is most likely a:

This is a false positive — the alert is technically correct (a port scan is happening), but it's from an authorized security tool performing its job. The detection rule should be tuned with an exception for this scanner's IP.

Q5. What is an Indicator of Compromise (IOC)?

IOCs are digital breadcrumbs — forensic evidence like malicious IP addresses, suspicious file hashes, unusual traffic patterns, or anomalous login behavior that indicate a breach may have occurred.

Q6. Which alert should be prioritized FIRST?

Ransomware on a production server is a critical threat requiring immediate action. Every second of delay allows the ransomware to encrypt more data and potentially spread to other systems.

Q7. In the SOC simulation, which screen shows the global threat map and network traffic?

Screen 2 is the Network Monitor — it displays the global threat map, live traffic graphs, firewall logs, top talkers, and port activity, giving you visibility into network-level threats.

Q8. What does "Lateral Movement" mean in the ATT&CK framework?

Lateral Movement (TA0008) is when an attacker moves from one compromised system to others within the network, using stolen credentials, remote services, or shared drives to pivot deeper into the environment.

Q9. What is the primary purpose of EDR (Endpoint Detection and Response)?

EDR solutions are installed on endpoints (laptops, servers, etc.) to monitor processes, file changes, and network connections in real time, detect suspicious behavior, and enable rapid response like isolating compromised machines.

Q10. During an investigation, you discover a suspicious DNS query from an internal workstation to a newly registered domain. Screen 4's threat feed confirms this domain is linked to a known threat group. What is the correct next step?

With a corroborated threat intelligence match, this is a confirmed true positive. The correct procedure is to open a formal incident case on Screen 3, document all IOCs (the domain, the source IP, timestamps), and begin containment — typically by isolating the workstation to prevent further C2 communication.
